# How I use AI in my design process

In the [previous article](/why-i-design-again), I wrote about coming back to design after realizing I was confusing activity with progress. Now I do the thinking before I prompt the swarm.

But here's what I've found: AI is good at the thinking part too.

### AI as a thinking partner

The hardest part of design isn't drawing screens. It's making decisions under uncertainty. Who is this for? What problem am I actually solving? Why this approach over the alternatives?

I've started using AI to help me think through these questions.

**Research.** I have it summarize articles, studies, and expert opinions. I ask it to look up existing solutions that tackle similar problems. It gets me to a baseline understanding faster than I could on my own.

**Idea generation.** I ask it to frame the same problem three different ways. I ask it to argue against my current approach. It has no ego in the outcome — it'll challenge my assumptions without politics, generate a solution I hate and one I hadn't considered. Both are useful. The first sharpens my judgment, the second expands my options.

One thing I've learned to watch for: AI defaults to being supportive. It'll validate a mediocre idea if I present it with confidence. I've gotten better results by explicitly asking it to poke holes, to tell me what I'm missing, to argue the other side.

**Framework generation.** This is where it gets powerful. I ask AI to help me build a framework for evaluating my solutions — the dimensions that matter, the trade-offs I need to weigh, the criteria for "good enough." Then I check: does this framework cover all the corners? What's missing?

Then I ask it to build a *different* framework that approaches the problem from another angle entirely.

Now I'm not just generating options. I'm generating *ways of seeing the problem*. A framework that optimizes for user experience will surface different winners than one that optimizes for technical simplicity or time to market. Having multiple lenses before I commit is the whole point.

### AI as a design worker

Some design work is thinking. Some is labor.

Synthesizing research into patterns. Mapping out user flows. Documenting constraints. Writing acceptance criteria. These are things that take hours and get skipped because they're tedious — not because they're unimportant.

AI handles this well. I give it my research notes and ask for a synthesis. I give it my problem frame and ask for user journey maps. I give it my list of possible solutions and ask it to evaluate each against my constraints in a structured way.

This is where AI goes from thinking partner to *design infrastructure*. It does the work that makes good decisions possible — the work I used to skip when under pressure to start building.

### Where I use it in practice

During the *empathize* and *define* phases:
- who are the users, what do they actually need
- what does the problem look like from their side
- what am I assuming that I haven't validated

During *ideation*:
- generate multiple distinct solutions at different scales
- explore trade-offs before committing to one
- stress-test each against my constraints

During *evaluation*:
- create a decision framework, then pressure-test it for blind spots
- build an alternative framework from a different angle
- map my solutions against both and see what holds up

### The best time to use AI

I used to reach for AI when it was time to build. That's the second-best time.

The best time is when I'm still deciding *what* to build. The thinking is harder, the stakes are higher, and the cost of getting it wrong multiplies through everything that follows.

I use AI to think. Then I use AI to build what I thought through.
